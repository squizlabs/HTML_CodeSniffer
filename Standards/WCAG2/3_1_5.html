Success Criterion 3.1.5: Reading Level (Level AAA)

When text requires reading ability more advanced than the lower secondary education level after removal of proper names and titles, supplemental content, or a version that does not require reading ability more advanced than the lower secondary education level, is available.

(From Understanding WCAG 2.0: Understanding Success Criterion 3.1.5)

Discussion

Summary

The purpose of this Success Criterion is to ensure content is as easily readable as possible. The target level for content to satisfy Level AAA without additional supporting material is the UNESCO "lower secondary education" level, which concludes 9 years after starting primary (compulsory) education.

Many readability scales have been created, although automated tests are not simple as they require knowledge of the number of syllables words have. Most give their result as an approximate U.S. school grade level - scores beyond 12 indicating post-secondary education; beyond 15-16 indicating post-graduate level.

Generally speaking, sufficiently readable passages of text produce a figure of 9 or lower. For example, the Abstract of the Understanding WCAG 2.0 document, taken as a document in itself, would probably not satisfy this Success Criterion by itself, as most readability scores place it at about the (U.S.) Grade 10-11 level [estimate derived from readability-score.com].

If the readability guideline cannot be met, supplementary information should be made available. This could include:

  • an alternative summary at the required reading level, or
  • providing additional diagrams or iilustrations to explain difficult portions of the text, or
  • providing a spoken version of the text, or
  • providing a sign language version of the text (in cases where sign language may be such users' first language, not English).

In Australia, the appropriate stage is Year/Grade 9. UNESCO does not appear to consider the "Foundation Year" (generally known as Kindergarten, Grade Prep, or other terms depending on state) as "primary education", even if it is often done in a primary school, as it is not always compulsory in all states.
