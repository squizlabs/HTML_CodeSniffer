Success Criterion 1.3.2: Meaningful Sequence (Level A)

When the sequence in which content is presented affects its meaning, a correct reading sequence can be programmatically determined.

(From Understanding WCAG 2.0: Understanding Success Criterion 1.3.2)

Discussion

Summary

The purpose of this Success Criterion is to ensure that, where passages of content need to be linearised, the meaning of the content does not change. This is important for users who need to read the page in a linear order, such as those using screen readers, or those that need to replace the default styling with an alternate style sheet.

A good example is that of a piece of main content that is presented in three columns, like a newspaper. To satisfy this Success Criterion, the programmatic reading order of the page would need to flow from the end of one column to the next, in the direction of reading (left to right in English). It is possible to produce alternative recreations of such a styling that do not produce such a reading order, and these would not result in satisfying this Success Criterion.

Does sequence always have meaning?

In some cases, changing the order of a sequence of items would not affect the meaning of each of them. The Understanding document provides the example of a main story, an independent side story, and a navigation bar on the same page. These items could be placed independently of each other and not make any difference to the meaning of each. Therefore, this type of order would not require the satisfaction of this Success Criterion.
