Success Criterion 1.4.2: Audio Control (Level A)

If any audio on a Web page plays automatically for more than 3 seconds, either a mechanism is available to pause or stop the audio, or a mechanism is available to control audio volume independently from the overall system volume level.

Note: Since any content that does not meet this success criterion can interfere with a user's ability to use the whole page, all content on the Web page (whether or not it is used to meet other success criteria) must meet this success criterion. See Conformance Requirement 5: Non-Interference.

(From Understanding WCAG 2.0: Understanding Success Criterion 1.4.2)

Discussion

Why is failure considered interference?

This Success Criterion comes with a warning about failure to meet the criterion counting as "interference", thus failing Conformance Requirement 5 even if such audio technology is not relied upon to achieve WCAG 2.0 conformance.

There are two main reasons for this being considered interference:

  • Automatic sounds may interfere with the understanding of the page of those users relying on a screen reader or similar techology, if only because they will likely play at the same volume, and thus make the content less accessible to such users. Any method to control (rather than mute) the audio must include the ability for it be reduced to zero.

  • Further, for users relying on a screen reader or similar technology, they are effectively navigating by sound, and thus the order in which they hear things is important. If a sound automatically plays, it may be thought to be in a different location to where it may be (if it is visible at all). This may interfere with their ability to navigate to the source of the sound and turn it off.

The W3C Understanding document recommends that sounds be started by user action, rather than automatically. An exception is provided for those sounds shorter than three seconds.
