Success Criterion 1.4.1: Use of Colour (Level A)

Colour is not used as the only visual means of conveying information, indicating an action, prompting a response, or distinguishing a visual element.

Note: This success criterion addresses colour perception specifically. Other forms of perception are covered in Guideline 1.3 including programmatic access to colour and other visual presentation coding.

(From Understanding WCAG 2.0: Understanding Success Criterion 1.4.1)

Discussion

Summary

The Success Criterion is fairly self-explanatory, and is meant to provide assistance to users with forms of colour-blindness, as well as those users with other visual deficiencies. Although use of colour is not discouraged, redundant indicators of changed state or meaning of text ensure that these groups of users do not miss out on the information.

Examples include:

  • Labels on form controls are coloured red to indicate they are required. To provide redundant information, the labels also have include the text "(required)". This means that this information is programmatically associated with the required control, ensuring that poorly-sighted users have easy access to this information.

  • A public transport web site has an indicator for each train line showing a green, orange or red light depending on whether trains on that line are on time, delayed or suspended. To provide redundancy, a short summary (eg. "On Time") appears next to the "traffic light" image. Simply adding alt text to the image would be insufficient because it would not be available to colour-blind users reading the web site visually.

  • A pie graph uses different colours for each slice, but also provides a slightly different pattern which is discernable to those with colour deficiencies, allowing them to still associate the slices with their description.
