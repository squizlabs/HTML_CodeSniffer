Success Criterion 2.2.2: Pause, Stop, Hide (Level A)

For moving, blinking, scrolling, or auto-updating information, all of the following are true:

  • Moving, blinking, scrolling: For any moving, blinking or scrolling information that (1) starts automatically, (2) lasts more than five seconds, and (3) is presented in parallel with other content, there is a mechanism for the user to pause, stop, or hide it unless the movement, blinking, or scrolling is part of an activity where it is essential; and
  • Auto-updating: For any auto-updating information that (1) starts automatically and (2) is presented in parallel with other content, there is a mechanism for the user to pause, stop, or hide it or to control the frequency of the update unless the auto-updating is part of an activity where it is essential.

*Note 1: For requirements related to flickering or flashing content, refer to Guideline 2.3.
*Note 2: Since any content that does not meet this success criterion can interfere with a user's ability to use the whole page, all content on the Web page (whether or not it is used to meet other success criteria) must meet this success criterion. See Conformance Requirement 5: Non-Interference.
*Note 3: Content that is updated periodically by software or that is streamed to the user agent is not required to preserve or present information that is generated or received between the initiation of the pause and resuming presentation, as this may not be technically possible, and in many situations could be misleading to do so.
*Note 4: An animation that occurs as part of a preload phase or similar situation can be considered essential if interaction cannot occur during that phase for all users and if not indicating progress could confuse users or cause them to think that content was frozen or broken.

(From Understanding WCAG 2.0: Understanding Success Criterion 2.2.2)

Summary

The purpose of this Success Criterion is to provide the ability to stop moving or auto-updating content so that users with various difficulties can read the content. These groups of people include:

  • Users who have visual difficulties in reading moving content,
  • Slow readers, who may have difficulty reading moving or auto-updating content in the allowed time before it goes off the screen,
  • Those reading with screen readers, which may attempt to re-read auto-updating content,
  • Those users with attention deficit disorders, where the blinking may be considered a distraction.

The working group chose five seconds for the distraction reason, because "it is long enough to get a user's attention, but not so long that a user cannot wait out the distraction if necessary to use the page".

Automatic updates can either resumed either from the point the user left off, or from the latest piece of information. Whichever is appropriate will depend on the purpose of the content: some content will be appropriate for one method or the other.

Blinking vs. Flashing

The Understanding document delineates what is considered a "blink" and what is considered a "flash". Blinking in this case refers to content that can be a distraction, whereas flashing is content that can cause a seizure, as per Success Criterion 2.3.1.

Interference

You will notice a point about Conformance Requirement 5 regarding non-interference. The reasoning behind this is that some groups of users, such as those with attention deficit disorders or those using screen readers, may find it difficult to handle the appearance of auto-updating or blinking text to the point where the constant distraction interferes with being able to understand the entire page.

This makes it important for this Success Criterion to be applied to all content, regardless of whether or not it is relied upon to conform.
