Success Criterion 1.4.5: Images of Text (Level AA)

If the technologies being used can achieve the visual presentation, text is used to convey information rather than images of text except for the following:

  • Customizable: The image of text can be visually customized to the user's requirements;
  • Essential: A particular presentation of text is essential to the information being conveyed.

Note: Logotypes (text that is part of a logo or brand name) are considered essential.

(From Understanding WCAG 2.0: Understanding Success Criterion 1.4.5)

Note

Superseded by Success Criterion 1.4.9 at level AAA, which removes most of the exceptions.

Summary

The purpose of this Success Criterion is to ensure that as much information that could be made available as text, is available as text rather than as an image of text.

The most obvious reasoning is to make the text most accessible to all users. This is not necessarily so the text can be read out to assistive technology such as a screen reader: there are text alternative techniques (such as alt text and long descriptions) that cater for this, although there are advantages in this area to avoiding images of text where possible.

The main reasoning given in the Understanding document is for visual handicaps: providing text instead of an image of text allows users to adjust the size, spacing and other formatting to suit their needs. Not all of this is possible with an image - it can be resized using zoom in most modern user agents, but this does not allow for customisation of other formatting, such as line spacing.

Exceptions

There are a number of exceptions:

  • Images of text are permitted where the effect would not be reliably presented in commonly available user agents. For instance, where a typeface is not commonly redistributed, and is not licensed for further distribution (for instance, in WOFF or EOT format).

  • They are permitted where implementing as text would interfere with other Success Criteria.

    Example: implementing shadowed text. The text-shadow CSS feature (in standard form at least) is not available in all browsers. It is sometimes emulated, for instance, through positioning a second layer with the same text slightly behind the original. However, it could be argued that this would not be an accessibility-supported method of implementing shadowed text: those relying on assistive technology such as a screen reader may find that it reads such shadowed information twice, causing issues similar to "link stutter". In this case, an image of text could potentially be argued for.

  • As is common in WCAG 2.0, exceptions exist for text considered "essential", such as branding inside a logotype, or high-quality samples of a typeface.

Also take note of the definition of "images of text":

The definition of image of text contains the note: "Note: This does not include text that is part of a picture that contains significant other visual content." Examples of such pictures include graphs, screenshots, and diagrams which visually convey important information through more than just text.

Understanding WCAG 2.0: Images of Text: Understanding SC 1.4.5

Note: most of these exceptions do not apply when targeting the AAA level, where Success Criterion 1.4.9: Images of Text (No Exceptions) applies. Contrary to the name of the criterion, there are some exceptions, but visual presentation cannot be used as an excuse.

Technologies other than HTML

This Success Criterion applies regardless of the technology involved. For instance, PDF documents with text can either be saved with the text preserved (so it can be selected, for instance), or as an image. Where possible, the document should be saved so the text is preserved and accessible.
