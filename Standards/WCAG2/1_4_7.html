Success Criterion 1.4.7: Low or No Background Audio (Level AAA)

For prerecorded audio-only content that (1) contains primarily speech in the foreground, (2) is not an audio CAPTCHA or audio logo, and (3) is not vocalization intended to be primarily musical expression such as singing or rapping, at least one of the following is true:

  • No Background: The audio does not contain background sounds.
  • Turn Off: The background sounds can be turned off.
  • 20 dB: The background sounds are at least 20 decibels lower than the foreground speech content, with the exception of occasional sounds that last for only one or two seconds.

Note: Per the definition of "decibel," background sound that meets this requirement will be approximately four times quieter than the foreground speech content.

(From Understanding WCAG 2.0: Understanding Success Criterion 1.4.7)

Note

The note may be confusing (as normally a 3 dB increase in power in anything, such as signal strength, is a 2x increase), but psychoacoustic models suggest a 10x increase in sound pressure (hence, 10 dB) is required for a 2x increase in loudness.

Discussion

Summary

The main purpose of this Success Criterion is to ensure that speech is understandable for users that are hard of hearing. The main problem this class of users have is difficulty in distinguishing noises, such as speech from its background noise. By eliminating background noise, or at least having a clear deliniation between the two when audio media is mixed, it may become easier for the hard-of-hearing to understand the content.

Exceptions for musical expressions are provided, as well as audio CAPTCHAs, and audio logos, as well as occasional sounds that do not linger. Audio logos - essentially branding a product or entity through sound - are considered "essential" like other branding (such as visual logotypes) and thus are afforded similar exceptions.

Why 20 dB?

The figure of 20 dB was taken from reports regarding persons who hard of hearing, and what is a sufficient signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for certain applications: one report, for instance, relates to a recommendation to the U.S. Access Board (in 1999) for large area assistive listening systems, such as hearing induction loops for persons with hearing aids - sometimes called "T-coils" after the position on the aid used to activate it, usually called the "T-switch".

As these are often used in busy areas, the volume level produced by the loop should be loud enough to rise sufficiently above the background noise; the report in this instance recommended a SNR of 18 dB. The figure of 20 dB is easier to work with as this produces a round multiple of loudness (of four times) to aim for.

Notices

Message Name Applies To Techniques
